20 Day English Fluency Plan: Day 1 – Modals: Obligation, Advice & Necessity

May 14, 2025

Welcome to Day 1 of the 20 Day English Fluency Plan! This series is designed to boost your spoken English and grammar skills, helping you communicate with confidence. Today, we dive into modals—words like must, have to, should, and ought to—that express obligation, advice, and necessity. Through a travel-inspired story, a real-life conversation, and a reflective prompt, you’ll learn to use modals naturally.

The Backpacker’s Code

When you're traveling solo, you must always stay aware of your surroundings. You have to keep copies of important documents—just in case. Locals might advise that you should avoid walking alone late at night, even in safe cities. And while it’s not a rule, you ought to respect local customs—it's a sign of good manners.

On my last trip to Japan, I had to figure out public transport quickly. I realized I should have learned some basic phrases beforehand. Still, the kindness of strangers reminded me: you must stay open and adaptable. That’s what travel teaches you—what really matters, and what can wait.

Words in Action: Notice how modals shift tone—must and have to signal strict necessity, should have expresses regret, and should or ought to offer gentle advice. For detailed concepts and usage tables, refer to your study materials.

Real-Life Conversation: Convince Your Roommate

Scenario: Your roommate keeps leaving dishes in the sink and forgetting to pay bills on time. Use modals to have a polite but clear conversation.

Example Dialogue (Your Role):

  • Hey, I think we should talk about a few things.
  • You have to understand it affects both of us.
  • I’m not trying to be harsh, but we must keep the kitchen clean—it’s basic hygiene.
  • You really ought to set calendar reminders for bills. Want help with that?

Your Turn (Respond as the Roommate): Practice responding with modals. For example:

  • I know I should be more organized.
  • I have to admit, I’ve been forgetting the dishes.
  • You’re right, I must be more mindful.
  • Maybe I ought to create a checklist.

Try this dialogue aloud with a friend or in front of a mirror to build confidence. Focus on tone—be firm yet friendly.

Wrap-up Reflection

Let’s end with a thought-provoking prompt to keep using modals:

What are three things we often should do, but avoid? What are two things society says we must do, but we question?

Discuss these questions with a partner, write a short reflection, or think about them quietly. Use modals to express your ideas (e.g., “I should exercise more, but I avoid it because…”). This exercise helps you apply modals in meaningful ways.

Next Steps

Tomorrow, we’ll explore another key aspect of English fluency to keep building your skills. Practice today’s modals in conversations or writing, and check your study materials for additional speaking and writing tasks. See you for Day 2!
