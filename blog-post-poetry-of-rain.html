The Poetry of Rain: Chaos, Stillness, and Becoming

March 22, 2025

The skies darkened before their time today in my hometown — Rourkela, Odisha. Winds howled through the trees, lifting the dust into restless spirals before the heavens finally opened. Thunder cracked, lightning streaked across the sky, and then came the rain — wild, urgent, cleansing.

Kal Baisakhi. A name that carries the pulse of the land, a paradox of destruction and renewal, storm and stillness, chaos and rebirth. It arrives unannounced, fierce and unapologetic, tearing through the heat of summer, only to leave behind a world washed clean, glistening in the aftermath.

As I sit on my apartment terrace, the cool wind rushes past me, carrying the scent of wet earth, the whispers of something ancient and eternal. My skin tingles from the chill, yet I can’t stop smiling. This rain feels like a gift, a moment suspended between all that was and all that will be.

I have longed for this — to simply be, to listen, to feel. For weeks, I have been wrapped in movement, in preparations, in the rhythm of change. And yet, like the dust before the storm, my thoughts have swirled without settling.

I think of my journey — the years that shaped me, the experiences that led me here. Thirty years of life, of seeking, of becoming, all converging into now. I feel gratitude swelling in my heart — for the universe, for love, for the partner who has walked into my life like a song I had always longed to hear. He is everything I have ever wanted, everything I have dreamed of, and even more — things I never knew I would need. He is my true symphony, teaching me the art of simply being, holding me close in a way that speaks of quiet understanding, of unwavering presence.

As the rain falls and the wind sings, I promise myself this: I will honor his love. I will cherish his family as my own. I will step into this new journey with open arms, with devotion woven into every moment, every bond, every act of love.

The dusky sky shifts, the clouds moving in their endless waltz. Birds soar in the wind’s embrace, the distant mountains stand watchful, and the trees — half adorned in autumn’s farewell, half bursting with new leaves — bear witness to my presence, my becoming.

And in this moment, I understand.

Life, like Kal Baisakhi, moves in rhythms of storm and stillness, of endings and beginnings, of destruction and renewal. And just as the earth surrenders to the storm, trusting that it will bloom anew, I surrender to this moment, this change, this beautiful unknown.

The storm has passed, but something within me has awakened.
